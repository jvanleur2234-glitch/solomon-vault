## Key Findings

### YouTube's Own AI Slop Detection (April 2026)
- YouTube is testing a feature that asks viewers: "Does this video feel like AI slop?"
- This appears as a quick poll in the rating interface
- YouTube is outsourcing detection to users because automated tools are not good enough
- The gap between human detection and AI capability is widening fast
- YouTube is also purging some AI slop channels, but the problem persists at scale
- Nothing stops YouTube from using this feedback to train future AI models

### AI Content Declaration Policy (2024)
- YouTube requires creators to declare if content is AI-generated
- The platform will then label such videos
- This started in 2024 and is enforced more strictly in 2026

### AI-Generated Channel Networks (DFRLab, March 2026)
- 26 YouTube channels coordinated network publishing AI-generated political content
- Nearly 2 billion total views combined
- Uses synthetic AI voiceovers and AI-generated news anchors
- Content mimics legitimate news reports with fabricated events
- No disclosure that visuals or narration are artificially generated

### Detection Tools Available
Hive, Sentinel, Deepware, and similar platforms offer AI video detection. Hive provides API-based detection used by major platforms. Detection accuracy is still poor - even YouTube relies on crowdsourced viewer feedback because automated tools fail at scale.

### The Kids Content Implication
For the faceless kids YouTube channel:
- YouTube's declaration requirement means AI-generated content is tracked, not banned
- Viewer polls ("does this feel like AI slop?") matter for the algorithm
- The "Age of Authenticity" trend means humanized content (real voices, real reactions) performs better
- Pure AI slop with no human element is increasingly penalized
